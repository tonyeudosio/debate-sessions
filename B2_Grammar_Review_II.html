1. Reported Speech

PatternUse + Example
StatementsTense moves one step back → "I am tired," she said. → She said she was tired.
QuestionsNo auxiliary, normal word order → "Where do you live?" → She asked where I lived.
Yes/No questions+ if / whether → "Can you help me?" → She asked if I could help her.
Commands & requeststell/ask + object + (not) + infinitive → "Don't touch that." → He told us not to touch it.
Time/place changestomorrow→the next day, yesterday→the day before, here→there

2. Conditionals

TypeFormula + Example
ZeroIf + present, present (general truth) → If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
FirstIf + present, will + infinitive (real future possibility) → If it rains, we'll cancel the picnic.
SecondIf + past simple, would + infinitive (unreal present/future) → If I had more time, I would travel more.
ThirdIf + past perfect, would have + p.p. (unreal past) → If she had studied harder, she would have passed.

3. Relative Clauses

TypeFormula + Example
DefiningNo commas; essential info → The woman who lives next door is a doctor.
Non-definingCommas; extra info → My brother, who works in Madrid, is visiting us.
whose (possession)The man whose car was stolen called the police.
where / when / whyThis is the restaurant where we met.
Omission of pronounWhen it's the object of a defining clause → The film (that) we watched was great.

4. Modals of Deduction & Obligation

ModalUse + Example
must (be)Logical certainty (positive) → He must be at home now.
can't (be)Logical certainty (negative) → She can't have seen the message.
might/may/could havePossibility → They might have missed the train.
have to / mustObligation → You must wear a seatbelt.
don't have to / needn'tNo obligation → You don't have to come if you're busy.
mustn'tProhibition → You mustn't smoke here.
should/shouldn'tAdvice → You should see a doctor.

5. Gerunds & Infinitives

PatternUse + Example
verb + -ingenjoy, avoid, mind, stop, look forward to → I enjoy reading before bed.
verb + to-infinitivedecide, manage, remember (future), agree → She decided to study abroad.
remember/forget + -ing vs to-inf.-ing = memory of the past; to-inf. = a task not yet done → I'll never forget seeing... / Remember to lock...
stop + -ing vs to-inf.stop -ing = finish an activity; stop to + inf. = pause in order to → He stopped smoking. / He stopped to smoke.

6a. Passive Voice — Personal & Impersonal

Impersonal Passive
It + passive reporting verb + that + clause
It is said that tourism brings money to the region.
👉 'It' is a dummy subject; the real subject stays inside the that-clause.
Personal Passive
Subject (from the that-clause) + passive reporting verb + to-infinitive
Tourism is said to bring money to the region.
👉 Use to + have + past participle when the action happened before the reporting verb: He is believed to have left the country.
ActivePersonal PassiveImpersonal Passive
People say tourism brings money to the region.Tourism is said to bring money to the region.It is said that tourism brings money to the region.
People believe the building is haunted.The building is believed to be haunted.It is believed that the building is haunted.
People know he worked as a chef.He is known to have worked as a chef.It is known that he worked as a chef.
⚠️ Most common reporting verbs: say, believe, think, know, consider, expect, report.

6b. Causative — have / get something done

PatternUse + Example
have + object + past participlePay/arrange for someone else to do something → I have my hair cut every month.
get + object + past participleSlightly more informal; also unfortunate events → She had her phone stolen.

6c. Passive with Two Objects

give / send / show / offer / tell + indirect object + direct object → either object can become the passive subject
ActivePassive (indirect object as subject)
They gave him a medal.He was given a medal.
They sent her an invitation.She was sent an invitation.
They offered her a new contract.She was offered a new contract.

6d. Common Passive Pitfalls

⚠️ Intransitive verbs (happen, arrive, occur, disappear) cannot be made passive: ❌ "The accident was happened." ⚠️ Don't forget the dummy "It" in the impersonal passive — without it, the sentence is incomplete.

7. Comparatives & Superlatives

PatternExample

comparative + and + comparativeThe traffic gets worse and worse every year.
nowhere near as... asThis phone is nowhere near as good as the previous model.
far / much + comparativeHe is far more experienced than his colleague.
