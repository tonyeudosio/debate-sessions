Before You Watch

to pontificate — to talk in a pompous, opinionated way
a buzzword — a fashionable word/phrase, often vague
to digress — to wander off the main topic
to bombard (someone with) — to overwhelm with a stream of things
to ramble — to talk at length without clear focus
a faux pas — a social mistake or embarrassing slip
to be on the same page (idiom) — to share the same understanding
to cut to the chase (idiom) — to get to the point quickly
Headlee speaks noticeably faster and more conversationally than Duckworth did — closer to real, unscripted speech. Don't expect to catch everything on the first pass; that's the whole point of today's session.

Quick check

In one or two sentences: what is Headlee's main message?

If this first watch felt noticeably harder than Session 1's, that's expected — and worth saying out loud rather than hiding. Naming the difficulty is itself part of building exam stamina.

Second Watch — Facts and Details

Headlee structures her talk as a numbered list — use that structure to help you track details, the way you'd use signposting language in the exam itself.

Detail · Personal anecdote

1. Headlee opens with a story about her grandfather. What was he known for?

Detail · Rule

2. One of her rules is "don't multitask." What reasoning does she give for this?

Detail · List recall

3. Name at least three of Headlee's ten rules, in your own words (you don't need her exact wording).

Detail · Specific example

4. She mentions a specific (and slightly embarrassing) personal example of "pontificating" or talking too much. What was it, as far as you caught it?

Detail · Numbers

5. Catch at least one number or statistic she mentions (it could relate to surveys, studies, or anything else) and what it refers to.

Third Watch — Reading Between the Lines

As before, no auto-checking here — we'll talk these through together. The goal is noticing implication, tone, and rhetorical choices.

Inference · Self-deprecation

6. Headlee admits to her own conversational failures more than once. What effect do you think this has on the audience, rather than if she simply gave advice without admitting her own mistakes?

Inference · Implication

7. Without saying it outright, what does Headlee suggest about how technology and social media have changed the way we converse?

"Go out, talk to people, listen to people. And, most importantly, be prepared to be amazed."

Inference · Closing line

8. This is her closing line. Why do you think a speaker chooses to end on an instruction ("go out, talk...") rather than a summary of what was already said?

Speaking

Same escalating structure as before — but Task C now asks you to sustain an argument for slightly longer, with a clearer counter-argument expected.

Task A · Summarising (easiest)

Summarise three of Headlee's ten rules for someone who hasn't watched the talk, and briefly say why each one matters.

Try using at least two words or expressions from today's vocabulary list naturally.

Task B · Personal connection

Which of her ten rules do you personally struggle with most — in English, in Spanish, or both? Give a real example.

Self-deprecating honesty is a strength here, not a weakness — Headlee herself uses it constantly.

Task C · Taking a position

Headlee partly blames modern technology for our declining conversational skills. Do you agree, or do you think this is an old complaint that gets recycled with every new generation of technology? Argue your position for about 90 seconds, and address at least one counter-argument.

Structure: claim → reason → acknowledge the other side → conclusion. This is almost exactly CAE Speaking Part 4 territory.
