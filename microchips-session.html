Before Anything Else — A 30-Second Warm-Up

Just talk · no right answer

Before we even look at today's topic: tell me about something ordinary that happened since our last session. What did you have for breakfast? How was the traffic? Anything at all.

This doesn't get checked, scored, or corrected. Its only job is to get your voice moving in English before the "real" tasks start.

Before You Watch

microchip / chip — tiny electronic device with circuits
silicon — the material chips are mostly made from
wafer — a thin slice of silicon
circuit — a path that electricity flows through
etching — cutting a precise pattern into a surface
clean room — an ultra-dust-free factory space

Second Watch — Hunting for Details

This time we're going a little further than last session — a few more questions, and a couple ask you to connect two pieces of information rather than just spot one fact. Replay as much as you like; that's part of the method, not a failure.

Detail · Material

1. What is the main raw material microchips are made from?

Detail · Process

2. What is the purpose of the "clean room" mentioned in the video?

Detail · Open answer

3. The video mentions an environmental concern related to chip manufacturing. What is it, as far as you understood?

Detail · Numbers

4. Catch one number from the video (size, quantity, percentage, year — anything) and what it refers to.

Detail · Connecting two facts

5. A bit harder: explain in your own words, in 1–2 sentences, how a flat piece of silicon becomes a working chip with circuits on it. You don't need every step — just the general idea, connecting what happens first to what happens after.

Speaking — Using What You Heard

Same principle as last time: you're recycling language and facts you've just processed, not inventing from nothing. Task C asks slightly more of you than last session — a short opinion, not just description.

Task A · Retelling (easiest)

Explain to a colleague, in your own words, how microchips are made — as if they know nothing about it.

Tip: start with "It starts with a material called silicon..." — the shape of the explanation matters more than perfect detail.

Task B · Professional connection

In your own engineering work, do you ever deal with anything related to electronics, materials, or precision manufacturing? Tell me briefly how it connects (or doesn't) to what you just watched.

If there's no direct connection, that's a perfectly fine answer too — just say so and explain why.

Task C · A small opinion

The video raises an environmental concern about chip production. Do you think this is something companies take seriously enough? Just a short, honest reaction — 30 seconds is plenty.

There's no "correct" opinion here. This task is really about getting comfortable saying "I think..." out loud in English.
