Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance

Angela Lee Duckworth · TED · ~6 minutes

Before You Watch

grit — sustained passion and perseverance toward a long-term goal
to plummet — to fall suddenly and steeply
stamina — the ability to sustain effort over time
aptitude — natural talent or ability for something
to falter — to lose strength or hesitate
a marathon, not a sprint (idiom) — a long sustained effort, not a short burst
to flounder — to struggle, lacking direction
a growth mindset — the belief that ability can be developed through effort
At C1 level, the goal isn't just recognising these words — it's noticing how the speaker uses some of them rhetorically, almost as small persuasive tools. Keep that in the back of your mind for the third watch.

Quick check

In one or two sentences: what is Duckworth's central argument?

Second Watch — Facts and Details

Replay as needed — at this level, the goal is precision, not just gist. Some answers require holding two details together, not just spotting one.

Detail · Background

1. What was Duckworth's job before she became a researcher?

Detail · Finding

2. In her classroom, what surprised her most about which students performed well?

Detail · Open answer

3. Duckworth describes several groups she studied to test her ideas about grit (e.g. West Point cadets, salespeople, students). Name at least two of these groups and what she found in each.

Detail · Numbers

4. Catch at least one specific statistic or figure she mentions, and what it refers to.

Detail · Honesty in the talk

5. What does Duckworth admit she does NOT yet know how to do, near the end of the talk?

Third Watch — Reading Between the Lines

This is the C1 layer: not just what she says, but how and why she says it. These questions ask for inference — there isn't always one "correct" wording, so we'll discuss your answers together rather than auto-checking them.

Inference · Tone

6. Why do you think Duckworth chooses to talk about her own uncertainty (what she doesn't know) rather than just presenting her findings with full confidence?

Inference · Implication

7. Without saying so directly, what does the talk suggest about how schools currently measure or reward students? What is she implicitly criticising?

"Grit is living life like it's a marathon, not a sprint."

Inference · Rhetorical effect

8. This is one of the most quoted lines from the talk. Why do you think it works so well as a soundbite — what makes it memorable, beyond just being true?

Speaking

Tasks build from description to argument — the last one asks you to take a position, which is exactly the kind of speaking the CAE exam will eventually ask of you, but without exam pressure today.

Task A · Summarising (easiest)

Summarise Duckworth's argument for someone who hasn't seen the talk — what is grit, and why does she think it matters more than talent alone?

Try to use at least two words from today's vocabulary list naturally.

Task B · Personal connection

Think of someone you know — or yourself — who succeeded through sheer perseverance rather than natural talent. Tell that story briefly.

This is a classic CAE-style prompt: personal anecdote used to illustrate an abstract idea.

Task C · Taking a position

Duckworth admits she doesn't fully know how to teach grit. Do you think perseverance can actually be taught, or is it mostly a fixed personality trait? Argue your view for about a minute, and address at least one counter-argument.

Try structuring it like a mini essay: claim → reason → acknowledgment of the other side → conclusion.
